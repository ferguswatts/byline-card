How We Score

Complete transparency in our methodology — every step is open source

1

Article Discovery

We crawl sitemaps from NZ's major news outlets to build a comprehensive index of every published article. For each URL, we extract the author name using outlet-specific strategies — APIs, structured data, or streaming HTML.

Outlets: NZ Herald, Stuff, RNZ, The Spinoff, Newstalk ZB, 1News, Newsroom
2

Text Extraction

Full article text is extracted using trafilatura, an open-source library designed for web content extraction. The extracted text is cleaned of navigation, ads, and boilerplate before scoring.

Minimum threshold: Articles under 200 characters are excluded as likely stubs or fragments
3

AI Scoring

Each article is scored by Claude Sonnet 4.5 (Anthropic) on a scale from -1.0 (hard left) to +1.0 (hard right). The AI evaluates five dimensions of bias for each article and applies the "Cui Bono" principle — asking who benefits from the story being published.

Model: claude-sonnet-4-5  |  Prompt version: v2-cuibono
4

Aggregation

Individual article scores are aggregated into five buckets for each journalist. The spectrum position shown on each card is the median score across all their articles from the past 24 months. Confidence ratings (low/medium/high) reflect the number of articles scored.

Buckets: Left (<-0.6) · Centre-Left (-0.6 to -0.2) · Centre (-0.2 to 0.2) · Centre-Right (0.2 to 0.6) · Right (>0.6)

The Five Dimensions of Bias

Every article is evaluated across these five dimensions, each scored independently:

◀
Story Selection

The editorial choice of what to cover. Publishing a story about one party's scandal while ignoring the other's is itself a signal — even before a word is written.

■
Framing

How is the topic presented? Who is positioned as protagonist or antagonist? The frame shapes reader perception before they process the facts.

▶
Source Selection

Which voices are quoted? Are opposing views included? Over-representing one side's experts or spokespeople creates an imbalanced narrative.

▲
Language

Loaded words like "slammed", "controversial", "radical", or "common sense" inject editorial judgement into ostensibly neutral reporting.

▼
Omission

What relevant context is missing? Profiling a politician's controversial views while omitting their actual policy record is bias by absence.

You are a political bias analyst for New Zealand media. Score the following
news article on a scale from -1.0 (hard left) to +1.0 (hard right).

Consider these dimensions:
- FRAMING: How is the topic presented? Who is the protagonist/antagonist?
- SOURCE SELECTION: Which politicians, experts, or voices are quoted?
- LANGUAGE: Is loaded or emotive language used?
- TOPIC EMPHASIS: What aspects are highlighted vs downplayed?
- OMISSION: What relevant context or perspectives are missing?

CRITICAL — ask "CUI BONO?" (who benefits from this story being published?):
- An article exposing a right-wing party's failures BENEFITS THE LEFT
- An article exposing a left-wing party's problems BENEFITS THE RIGHT
- The editorial choice of what to write about is itself a signal of lean
- "Both sides quoted" does NOT mean neutral

NZ political context:
- Left = Labour, Greens, Te Pati Maori
- Right = National, ACT
- Centre = NZ First (varies by issue)

Returns: score (-1.0 to 1.0), confidence, reasoning, and per-dimension scores.

Limitations & Caveats

  • AI is not infallible. Large language models can misinterpret sarcasm, cultural context, or NZ-specific political nuance. Individual article scores should be taken with a grain of salt — the aggregate pattern across hundreds of articles is what matters.
  • Bias is multidimensional. A single left-right spectrum is a simplification. Journalists may be progressive on social issues but conservative on economic policy. We use five sub-dimensions to partially address this.
  • Article availability varies. Not all articles are accessible for scoring. Some may be unavailable, removed, or behind access restrictions, which can affect representation.
  • Correlation is not intent. A journalist whose work scores left-leaning may be accurately reporting on a left-leaning government's policies. Context matters.
  • This is a starting point, not a verdict. Bias exists to surface patterns and encourage critical reading — not to label journalists as biased.
